Happy Birthday, Elisabeth Greffulhe

Tuesday July 1, 2018

This text should be centered below the image.

Born on this day in 1860, Elisabeth de Riquet de Caraman Chimay, comtesse Greffulhe, is one of the protagonists of my new triple biography, Proust’s Duchess: How Three Celebrated Women Captured the Imagination of Fin-de-Siècle Paris (Knopf 2018). Although countless artists and writers revered Elisabeth throughout her long life--she lived until 1952--her most famous admirer was, as my book title suggests, Marcel Proust, who came into the world almost exactly eleven years after she did, on July 10, 1871. Many decades later, Proust would turn her into one of the models for Oriane de Guermantes, duchesse de Guermantes in his seven-volume, loosely autobiographical masterpiece, A la recherche du temps perdu (1913-1927); and it is chiefly as an inspiration for that supremely elegant character (as well as for her similarly beguiling, if less frequently depicted, cousin, the princesse de Guermantes) that Elisabeth is remembered today.

As I show in my book, the fascination Elisabeth held for Proust began twenty years before he published the inaugural volume of the Recherche. He spotted her for the first time--from afar--at a society ball in Paris in the summer of 1893, when he was 22 and she 33. Afterward he recorded: “Never before have I seen a woman so beautiful.” As it happened, this view was shared by virtually everyone except her husband, the loutish and philandering Henry, comte Greffulhe, whose erotic preferences ran to blue-eyed blondes, and who had married the dark-haired, dark-eyed Elisabeth chiefly for her pedigree. Proust would later ascribe the couple’s marital dynamic to that of the duc and duchesse de Guermantes, describing Oriane as “the most beautiful, and the most cheated-on, woman in Paris.” For almost a year after this initial sighting, Proust pleaded with one of her kinsmen, comte Robert de Montesquiou Fezensac, to present him to her. Montesquiou, a highborn and very haughty gay aesthete who seemed to enjoy putting Proust, a mere commoner, in his place, repeatedly refused the request, once explaining with a sneer: "Do you not see that your presence in [the comtesse Greffulhe’s] salon would rob it of the very elegance you hope to find there?"

However, Montesquiou had an insatiable appetite for publicity, and in May 1894, Proust offered to help feed it by chronicling for a leading society newspaper an exclusive garden party the comte was planning to hold at the pavillon Montesquiou in Versailles. This was Proust’s way in. In order to write about the gathering, he would have to be invited to it, and since Elisabeth was to be the guest of honor, Montesquiou would be forced to introduce them: etiquette demanded that all guests be presented to the de facto queen of the occasion.

And so, on May 31, 1894, at the comte’s lordly, neoclassical residence in Versailles, Proust was granted his heart’s desire. Sweltering in his frock coat, top hat, and gloves beneath a blazing summer sun, he met Elisabeth at last. She had dressed for the party in a delicate, fashionably mauve confection by the great couturier Charles Frederick Worth, its silk skirt hand-painted with orchids and overlain with petal-pink tulle. In her chestnut-brown curls she wore a headdress trimmed with real orchids, and her face was wrapped--“like a precious fruit,” as she phrased it in one of her many unpublished writings--in an opaque, mauve veil. While these accessories did not survive, Elisabeth carefully preserved the Worth dress for posterity. After her death, her heirs gifted it to Paris’s Musee Galliera, which recently displayed it in an exhibition that subsequently traveled to the FIT museum in New York. Afterward, Montesquiou sniped that the color had not done much to flatter Elisabeth’s pale complexion (though in fact her face was barely visible beneath her veil). Proust, however, disagreed. For him, Elisabeth was nothing less than a rose-colored goddess, a sublime apparition barely, yet beautifully, made flesh. To Proust’s chagrin, the editor of the newspaper to which he contributed his account of the party substantially edited his love letter to patrician elegance. But a short description of Elisabeth’s outfit remained: “deliciously attired … .” This exquisite vision would haunt Proust’s consciousness for decades to come , drawing him ever onward into the imaginary, quintessentially noble realm of the "Guermantes Way."
